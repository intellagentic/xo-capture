II. Client Business Model & Scale
Francois Nembrini provided a highly detailed breakdown of his institutional Forex (Foreign Exchange) brokerage:
The Core Mechanism: The firm acts as an anonymous intermediary between major financial institutions. For example, if Lenders Bank Berlin wants to trade Euro/Dollar, Francois’s system matches them with a market maker like Goldman Sachs. The trade commercials are agreed upon on his system and then sent to a prime broker (e.g., Royal Bank of Scotland) to clear and deliver the currencies two days later.
Volume & Scale: The firm executes thousands of trades daily. The financial scale is massive, with their biggest single trading day hitting $4.5 billion.
Current Growth Initiatives: They currently serve 60–70 clients but have recently hired a sales team of 10 people to aggressively expand the client base.
Advanced Trading (Quants): They possess a small, highly advanced market-making function utilizing a team of 10 quants from Oxford and BNP Paribas, leveraging Fujitsu quantum computers and neural networks to find trading edges.
III. The Operational Bottleneck: Exceptions & Support
While the CRM and sales functions are deemed "too simple" for XO, Francois identified his critical operational bottleneck: the trade support and exceptions-handling function.
System Complexity: Automation handles most trades, but because they connect disparate bank systems, clearers, and credit facilities, things break daily.
Typical Exceptions: Issues include delayed trade confirmations, queues of unbooked trades, credit checks incorrectly blocking trades, fat-finger errors (traders booking under the wrong name requiring amendments), and clients not knowing how to use the interface. ALERTS 
The Financial Risk: The stakes for these exceptions are incredibly high. Francois notes that if an exposure is mismanaged, "You can lose $50,000, $100,000 in a couple of seconds." However, if the correct rapid triage action is taken, they never lose money.
IV. The Human Element & "Key Person" Dependency
Francois's business operates on a brutal 24/5 schedule (Sunday 5 PM New York time to Friday 5 PM New York time). This creates severe human capital challenges:
Training Time: It takes 2 to 3 years to train a support staffer from scratch (or 1 to 2 years with prior experience) because the exceptions are nuanced and historical.
Staff Breakdown & Dependency:
Tier 3 (Ultimate Escalation): Francois and his COO (20+ years of experience). They act as the ultimate safety net.
Tier 2: One highly competent junior trained over 10 years.
Tier 1 (Frontline): Two slower juniors (6–7 years tenure but operating at a 2–3 year knowledge level) and one newer junior (2–3 years tenure, struggles with English).
The Expansion Pain: As the new sales team brings on global accounts, the support volume will multiply across time zones. Francois cannot afford to send expensive London staff to Asia or the US to launch new desks. Currently, senior staff sleep next to their computers to handle night-shift escalations, resulting in virtually zero vacation time ("two days off a year").
