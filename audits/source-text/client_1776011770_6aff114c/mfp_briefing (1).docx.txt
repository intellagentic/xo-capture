  02   THE OPERATIONAL CRISIS: ANATOMY OF THE BOTTLENECK
To position the XO correctly, it is essential to understand what MFP's exception environment actually looks like — not in general terms, but in the specific mechanics of how breaks occur, what they cost, and why they resist conventional solutions.
How Breaks Occur
MFP operates as an intermediary across a constellation of banking systems, credit systems, and prime broker infrastructure that were not designed to speak to each other natively. Connectivity between these systems is maintained through a combination of proprietary API connections, standardised messaging protocols (FIX, SWIFT), and manual intervention where the protocols fail. At $4.5 billion daily peak volume, the failure rate — however small as a percentage — produces a significant absolute number of daily breaks.
Delayed confirmations: A trade executes but confirmation does not arrive from the counterparty or prime broker within the expected window. The position is live; the paper trail is not. Every second of delay is an unhedged exposure.
Unbooked trades: Trades that execute in the market but fail to post to MFP's internal books — typically caused by system connectivity timeouts, format mismatches between counterparty messaging, or capacity spikes that overflow the booking queue.
Fat-finger errors: Manual input errors — typically in notional amount, currency pair, or value date — that create a mismatch between what was intended and what is now sitting on the books. In institutional FX, a decimal-place error on a large notional is not a minor correction; it is an immediate P&L event.
Nostro/Vostro breaks: Discrepancies between MFP's expected cash positions and what is actually in their settlement accounts — often caused by timing differences, failed settlement, or counterparty reconciliation errors that surface at end-of-day but originate hours earlier.
Why the Asian Night Shift is the Critical Risk Window
The geography of FX trading means that the market does not pause when London goes to sleep. The Asian session — running roughly 23:00 to 08:00 London time — is a live trading window for MFP's institutional counterparties in Tokyo, Singapore, Hong Kong, and Sydney. Exceptions that occur in this window need to be resolved in this window. Unresolved, they compound.

  06   PROOF OF CONCEPT & NEXT STEPS
The Scoping Principle: Avoid Boiling the Ocean
Alan Moore's direction is precise and correct: the first XO deployment at MFP must be narrowly scoped. The instinct to solve every exception type simultaneously will produce a system that is broad, shallow, and unreliable. The correct approach is to build deep competence in a small number of exception types, validate that the XO's resolution outcomes match what Francois would have decided, and then expand.
Francois has been asked to identify the top 20 most frequent or highest-risk support issues. This is the right unit of scope for the POC. The selection criteria should apply a dual filter:
Frequency: High-frequency, routine exception types where the resolution procedure is well-established and the XO can immediately reduce the junior staff burden. These build confidence in the system quickly.
Risk weight: The exception types that, if mishandled, produce the largest P&L exposure. These may not be the most frequent, but their inclusion in the POC scope ensures the system addresses the highest-cost failure modes first.
